\section*{Question1}
\subsection*{1.}
A generalization due to Gnedenko and Kolmogorov states that the sum of a number of random variables with a power-law tail (Paretian tail) distributions decreasing as $|X|^{-\alpha-1}$ where$0<\alpha<2$(and therefore having infinite variance) will tend to a stable distribution $f(x,\alpha,0,c,0)$ as the number of summands grows. If $\alpha>2$, then the sum converges to a stable distribution with stability parameter equal to 2, i.e. a Gaussian distribution.\cite{bib1}\\
The mathematical expression of general CLT is
$$\frac{1}{\sqrt{nln(n)/12}}\sum\limits_{i=1}^nX_i-\mu \stackrel{d}{\longrightarrow} N(0,1)$$
Characteristic function of stable distribution:
$$f(s)=exp\{i\mu s-|cs|^\alpha[1-i\beta tan(\frac{\alpha \pi}{2})sgn(s)]\},\alpha \neq 1$$
$$f(s)=exp\{i\mu s-|cs|^\alpha[1-i\beta \frac{\pi}{2}log(s)sgn(s)]\},\alpha = 1$$

The central limit theorem states that the sum of a number of independent and identically distributed random variables with finite variances will tend to a normal distribution as the number of variables grows.

One implication for portfolio optimization is that the price for any asset is above or equal 0/is finite.

Under this restriction, we cannot use a infinite-variance stable distribution (i.e.,alpha peakedness parameter is less than 2.00), because an asset price less than 0/an infinite price is likely to occur. However, using Gaussian distribution to measure the yield rates and risks is neither accurate, because empirical distribution of assets are skewed and heavy-tailed.\cite{bib2}

More complicated models have been suggested to deal with this situation. Rachev and Menn (2005)have put forward appropriately truncated stable distributions.

Properties of the stable distribution: The stable distributions with alpha peakedness parameters less than 2.00 has infinite variance. The Gaussian Normal distribution (the “Bell Shaped Curve”) is a special case of the Stable Paretian distribution where the alpha peakedness parameter is 2.00.
